Deal Signal Map — Deal Insight's Proprietary Framework

The Deal Signal Map unifies what the industry has always split into two things — the qualification scorecard and the discovery narrative. In practice, discovery IS qualification. They're the same conversation captured in two formats because someone decided one is for the rep and one is for the manager. Deal Insight doesn't have that split. It's one brain.

Write the story. We'll score the deal.

Four signals. Four bars. Four colors. The logo IS the methodology.

1. The Pain — Why would they buy?

What's broken, what it's costing them, who it affects. AI scores whether the pain is documented, quantified, and confirmed by more than one person.

2. The Edge — Why us and not them?

Combines Value and Competition into one section. A rep prepping for a follow-up thinks "what do they care about, how are we better at it, and where are we not." That's one thought, one section. Why your solution wins and why the alternatives don't. Status quo is almost always the real competitor.

3. The People — Who matters?

Who decides, who champions, who blocks. Not just names and titles, but influence and sentiment. AI flags if you only have one contact or no executive access.

4. The Process — How do they buy?

Who has the checkbook, does legal get involved, how long does it take them to sign? One narrative: "Here's how this company buys things."

Every section feeds the Deal Score automatically. Reps don't "fill out a scorecard" — they capture what they know about the account, and Deal Insight extracts the signals and tells them where the gaps are. One input, two outputs: the rep gets their deal narrative, the manager gets their forecast confidence.

The Three Metrics

Signals are what the rep writes. Metrics are what the AI produces. Every open deal gets three numbers. They measure different things.

Deal Score (0-100)

How well do you know this deal? Rolls up from the four Signals using stage-weighted multipliers. A high score means you've done your homework. You know the pain, the people, the process. It does not mean the deal will close.

Deal Confidence (0-100%)

How likely is this deal to actually close? Combines Deal Score + Velocity + external factors: compelling events, budget reality, champion strength, close date stability. A deal can score 65 on knowledge and 25% on confidence. That means you know the deal well but it has no urgency.

Deal Value (ARR x Confidence%)

Is this deal worth your time? ARR comes from the price list (excludes implementation). Colored relative to the rep's target deal size, set by the manager. Prevents rewarding underperformance.

Vertex Solutions: $60K ARR x 15% confidence = $9K.
Meridian Group: $54K ARR x 45% confidence = $24.3K.

Vertex is the bigger deal. A rep would naturally chase it. But Meridian deserves nearly 3x more of your time because it can actually close. The math catches what gut instinct misses.

Deal Score = how well you know it. Deal Confidence = will it close. Deal Value = should you spend your time on it.

The Waterfall

Each layer feeds the next. The rep only touches the first layer. Everything else is calculated.

4 Signals → Deal Score → + Velocity → Confidence → x ARR → Value

The rep writes five boxes. The system produces three numbers. That's the whole product.

Evidence Scoring Hierarchy

Every claim a rep writes in Signals is rated on a four-level evidence hierarchy. Same words typed, completely different confidence.

LevelTypeMultiplierExample
1Rep Assertion0.25x"They need better reporting"
2Prospect Quote0.50x"She said she spends 2 hours a day pulling reports manually"
3Quantified Consequence0.75x"30% of her day gone. Can't do the analysis work she was hired for. Caused a rebate miss."
4Dollar Impact1.0x"They lost ~$100K last year from missed rebate tiers because nobody had time to analyze the data"

If a rep's Pain coverage is 80% but everything is Level 1, the actual score is 80% x 0.25 = 20. Same coverage at Level 4: 80% x 1.0 = 80. Same words typed, completely different confidence.

Stage-Weighted Scoring

Signal weights shift across deal stages so early deals aren't penalized for missing information they wouldn't have yet. A first-call deal missing Process knowledge still scores well. A late-stage deal missing Process gets hammered. Velocity (observed separately by the system) is not weighted here. It feeds Confidence directly.

Signal-by-Signal Scoring Rubric

Each signal is scored 0-100. The ranges below guide both the AI scoring and rep self-assessment.

1. The Pain (0-100)

0-20: No pain identified, or generic. 20-40: Problem described but no specifics on who or how. 40-60: Specific, affects named roles, but no cost attached. 60-80: Quantified (time lost, errors, manual hours). Consequence chain exists. 80-100: Dollar impact stated by prospect. Multiple people confirmed independently.

AI checks for: Specific problem described (+20). Tied to a business process (+15). Named roles affected (+15). Consequence if they don't fix it (+20). Dollar or time cost (+15). More than one person confirmed (+15).

Coaching if weak: "You've described a problem but the prospect hasn't told you what it costs. Try: 'Do you have a rough sense of what this is costing the business annually?'"

2. The Edge (0-100)

0-20: No desired outcome described, no awareness of alternatives. 20-40: Generic value proposition ("they want better visibility"), competitors named but no positioning. 40-60: Future state in prospect's words, evaluation criteria identified, but no dollar value or differentiation in prospect's language. 60-80: Dollar impact estimated, solution linked to outcomes, you know why they'd pick you AND why they'd pick the competitor. 80-100: Prospect articulated the ROI themselves. Champion told you where competitors are weak. "Do nothing" risk assessed. Differentiation in their language, not yours.

AI checks for: Desired future state described (+10). In prospect's words, not rep's pitch (+10). Dollar value or measurable outcome (+15). Value connects to YOUR specific solution (+10). Are alternatives named (+10). Is "do nothing" assessed (+10). Are evaluation criteria known (+10). Differentiation in prospect's language (+10). Prospect compared you favorably (+15).

Coaching if weak: "You know their pain but haven't connected it to a dollar outcome or told them why you and not the other guy. Try: 'What are you comparing us against? What would make the decision obvious for you?'"

3. The People (0-100)

0-20: One contact, no decision structure understanding. 20-40: 2-3 contacts, roles and influence unclear. 40-60: Economic Buyer and Champion identified, sentiment not assessed. 60-80: Full map with EB, Champion, end users, blocker identified. Know what each cares about. 80-100: Champion tested and confirmed (took action). EB engaged directly. Blocker strategy exists.

AI checks for: More than one contact (+10). Economic Buyer identified by name (+20). Champion identified (+15). Champion has done something for you (+15). Blockers or skeptics identified (+10). Know what each stakeholder individually cares about (+15). Rep engaged at executive level (+15).

Coaching if weak: "You have one contact and no executive access. Try: 'Who else on your team would want to see this before moving forward?'"

4. The Process (0-100)

0-20: No understanding of how they buy. 20-40: Vague ("I think the VP approves it"). 40-60: Decision steps known, timeline and procurement missing. 60-80: Full process mapped with approval chain, legal, thresholds, timeline. 80-100: Process confirmed by prospect. Historical buying timeline referenced. Mutual close plan with dates.

AI checks for: Decision steps documented (+20). Approval chain named (+15). Legal/procurement involvement known (+15). Budget threshold for additional approvals (+15). Timeline based on past purchases (+15). Mutual action plan with dates (+20).

Coaching if weak: "Your close date is next month but you don't know their signing process. Try: 'When you've purchased software like this before, what did the approval process look like, and how long did it take?'"

Validation: Ken's Roofing Supply

The test: A real, active deal from Stacey's pipeline at Phocas Software. Ken's Roofing Supply, a two-branch roofing and building materials distributor in British Columbia, Canada. Scored against the Signals framework at every stage of the sales cycle, January 30 through March 13, 2026.

The data: Five Gong-recorded calls, a complete email thread (prospecting through close), internal prep notes, and a Mutual Action Plan. The AI scored each Signal after every interaction using the four-level evidence hierarchy.

What the test proved:

  1. The Signals framework captured what mattered at every stage. No critical gap was missed.
  2. The evidence hierarchy differentiated real knowledge from assumptions. A rep saying "they love us" scored differently than a prospect saying "this would save us $40K."
  3. Stage-weighted scoring prevented false flags. An early-stage deal missing Process info was not penalized for not knowing things it could not know yet.
  4. Coaching prompts would have changed behavior. After Feb 18, the AI would have told the rep to address the Epicor threat directly. That conversation never happened on a call, but the email thread resolved it anyway.
  5. Email data was as critical as call data. The pivotal moment (Feb 27, Alex's "CRAP" email after a bad Epicor demo) happened in email, not on a recorded call. A tool that only ingests calls would have missed the deal-defining moment entirely.
The deal's trajectory changed between calls, not during them. That's why email integration isn't optional.

Deal Forecast

Deal Rescue

Detects risk before the rep feels it. Signals: stage age exceeding norms, meeting cadence drop, stakeholder gaps, email engagement decline, Signal gaps, close date pushed, velocity decay, historical loss patterns. Each alert comes with a Rescue Playbook — specific next actions.

Example: Risk = champion only. Playbook: (1) Ask who else needs to sign off. (2) Introduce value recap. (3) Request joint meeting with economic buyer.

Example: The Edge Hail Mary. Deal is pushing? Stop asking for a "follow up." Send a 30-second video of the specific ROI gap you found in their discovery data. Don't beg for time. Deliver value that demands a response.

You said your deal is pushing? You mean your deal is dying.

Most tools stop at the alert, which is like an alarm you just hit snooze on. Deal Rescue doesn't just tell you something is wrong. It tells you exactly what to do next and gives you the time to be proactive instead of reactive. The difference between losing a deal and saving one is almost always timing.

Deal Card Architecture (approved March 22, 2026)

Two peer-level tabs at the top of each deal card:

DEAL tab: The data. Four Signals input boxes (colored left borders matching brand colors), contacts, meetings, company intelligence, deal notes, competitors, deal info. Anyone or anything fills this in: the rep typing notes, AI parsing a Gong transcript, email thread extraction, CRM import. It's the deal, not the rep.

SIGNALS tab: The intelligence. Everything AI-generated. The rep touches none of it. Equal weight, equal real estate. Uses darker purple #3C3489 for tab text and active underline.

1. Card header: Account name left, ARR right (neutral dark #333, never colored), stage label.
2. DEAL / SIGNALS peer tabs.
3. Four metric cards in a row (each unique visualization).
4. Four signal gradient bars (clickable accordion, one open at a time).

Metric Cards (4 across, each unique)

Deal Score: Pink donut ring. Conic gradient fill showing 0-100. Number centered inside. Ring = the grade.

Velocity: SVG pulse line. Teal when alive, amber when shifting, pink when dying. Six states: Surging (steep upward pulse), Active (healthy pulse), Cooling (flattening out), Flatline (straight pink line, dead/ghosted), Slipping (was alive, trending down), Reviving (was flat, signs of life). The line shape tells the story before you read the word. Signature feature.

Confidence: Purple gradient bar underneath the percentage number. Bar fill = confidence level. Matches signal bar visual language.

Value: Dual bar. Two side-by-side amber bars. Left bar (solid gradient, #F4D19C to #EF9F27) = Deal Value (ARR x Confidence%). Right bar (ghost amber, same gradient at 25% opacity with dashed border) = Total ARR. Dollar amounts displayed on each bar ($24K on solid, $54K on ghost). The gap between bar heights IS the visual. Tall ghost bar next to a short solid bar = low confidence, big opportunity cost. Nearly equal bars = deal is close to locked. No labels under bars needed. Solid vs ghost treatment communicates which is which. Replaces the single fill-from-bottom tank (approved March 22, 2026).

Signal Color Rule (resolved March 22)

Two color systems, two jobs, no confusion. The signal's brand color lives ONLY on the gradient bar and the "WHAT TO DO" label in coaching. Health status colors (pink=bad, amber=gaps, purple=progress, teal=strong) are NOT used on the signal bars. One color system per element. The bar handles identity. The length handles severity. ARR in the card header is always neutral dark gray. Signal colors never decorate. They always communicate.

Section 5

AI-Powered Intelligence

Deal Insight isn't just a tracker — it's an AI-powered deal coach that works alongside the rep at every stage of the sales cycle. The AI layer touches pre-call, live-call, post-call, and ongoing pipeline management.

ICP Definition & Prospecting

AI helps define and refine the Ideal Customer Profile based on win/loss patterns, deal attributes, and firmographic data. Once the ICP is dialed in, AI identifies which prospects in the database best match — so reps stop wasting time on accounts that were never going to close.

Pre-Call Planning

Before every call, AI prepares the rep with intelligence on the industry vertical, the prospect company, and individual contacts. Reps can upload past calls, emails, and prior communications — AI synthesizes everything so the rep walks in informed and armed for discovery or demo. No more scrambling through old notes five minutes before a call.

Post-Call Analysis

After every call, AI produces:

  • Signal scoring — automatically scored and updated across all four Signals
  • Discovery analysis — what we know, what we heard (in their words, with quotes), what they liked, what risks and blockers exist, what we don't know, and what to ask next
  • Call and deal summary — concise, shareable, ready for CRM or internal review

Sentiment & Next Steps

Based on call recordings and notes, AI objectively assesses customer sentiment and buying interest — not the rep's gut feeling, but data-driven analysis. From there, AI selects the appropriate playbook for next steps to move the deal forward, and assists with follow-up emails tailored to where the deal actually stands.

Objection Library

AI-powered objection handling built from real deal context. When a prospect pushes back, Deal Insight doesn't leave the rep scrambling — it serves up the right response based on the objection type, the deal stage, and the methodology the team runs. Format: The prospect said X → You say/do Y.

Examples:

  • "We need to think about it" → Isolate the real concern: "Totally understand. When you say think about it, is it the timing, the investment, or something we haven't addressed yet?"
  • "It's too expensive" → Reframe to value: "Compared to what? Let's look at what one rescued deal per quarter is worth to your team."
  • "We're happy with what we have" → Challenge the status quo: "Most teams are — until they see what they're missing. Can I show you what pipeline blind spots look like?"
  • "Send me some info" → Redirect to action: "Happy to — but the most useful thing I can send is specific to your situation. Can I ask two quick questions so I don't waste your time with a generic deck?"

The library grows over time as AI learns from what actually works across the team's deals — which responses led to next meetings, which killed momentum. Reps get smarter objection handling without memorizing scripts.

Proposal & Collateral Generation

AI produces branded proposals with pricing, relevant customer case studies, competitor comparisons, and a Mutual Action Plan — all generated from the deal context. No more copy-pasting from old decks at midnight before a deadline.

Rep Performance Scoring

AI objectively scores rep performance on sales proficiency based on call analysis — covering AE, AM, Pre-Sales, and CS interactions. This creates a training opportunity with specific suggestions for improvement. Scores can be sent privately to the rep for self-learning, or shared with leadership and sales enablement for guided coaching.

Automated CRM Sync

Reps never have to manually upload information into the system of record. All call notes, deal scores, deal summaries, and activity data are seamlessly pushed to the CRM for leadership visibility. The rep focuses on selling. The CRM stays clean. Everyone wins.

Ongoing Pipeline Temperature Monitoring

AI continuously monitors the full pipeline to direct reps on how to best use their time: which deals are real, which are going off track, when and how to move a deal forward, how to fix deals that can still be won, and how to cut bait on the ones that feel good but are a pipe dream wasting your time.

AI proactively issues alerts when critical milestones are in danger of being missed and deals are slipping — before the rep even realizes it.

The Philosophy

AI is doing a few things at once: monitoring the pipeline FOR the rep so they can focus on additional revenue generation, and providing an objective view of their pipeline that cuts through the optimism bias every seller carries. It's not replacing the rep — it's giving them superpowers.

Stop updating. Start prospecting. The AI handles the rest.

Methodology-Agnostic, Methodology-Aware

Deal Insight doesn't force one methodology. The AI looks at the deal stage, what's been covered, what's missing, the prospect's vertical, their history, and generates the right questions and talking points. Whatever framework your team runs, Deal Insight adapts.

Your methodology. Your deals. Your playbook — written for you.

Critical Gotchas

  • IndexedDB under file:// is scoped to exact file path. Renaming or moving the file loses all data. Always save new versions to the same filename and folder.

  • Contact photos live in IndexedDB, not the HTML file. They don't transfer when sharing the file. Use Save Backup/Load Backup JSON to migrate.

The Deal Signal Map is Deal Insight's proprietary framework. It is legally distinct from MEDDPICC® and other methodologies for several reasons:

Product Intelligence Principles

This section captures the connective tissue between features (Section 4), the AI layer (Section 5), and the roadmap (Section 13). It defines how things talk to each other, why they exist, and what we will never become.

Two-Layer AI

Deal Insight has two AI layers. They use the same data but do completely different jobs.

Layer 1: Rep-Side AI (the assistant). This AI works FOR the rep. It reads transcripts, emails, and call notes, then populates the rep's own card with structured data: contacts with names and titles, products discussed, next step with a date, meeting notes summarized, company intel updated, objections logged. The rep reviews and tweaks. They don't write from scratch.

Layer 2: Signals-Side AI (the analyst). This AI reads what's on the card and scores the deal. Deal Score, Velocity, Confidence, Value, per-signal coaching prompts. It tells the rep what's weak and what to do about it.

Every other AI sales tool scores the rep. Deal Insight's AI works FOR the rep.

The complete workflow: transcript or email goes in. Structured card fields get populated. Signals scores what's there. Coaching prompts tell the rep what to do next. CRM gets updated as a side effect through the API Bridge. The rep never opened Salesforce.

The Rep's Card IS the Data Model

The rep's workflow IS the data model. They're not feeding a system. They're working their deals, and the intelligence is a side effect.

AI reads everything on the rep card. Next step text and target date, timeline history, contacts, notes, close date, ARR, stage, Signals boxes. Every field. The card isn't a form the rep fills out for a manager. It's the data model the AI uses to score the deal. One input surface, two outputs: the rep gets their deal narrative, the manager gets their forecast.

Quiet Intelligence

Features that work without asking the rep to do anything. System-observed, not rep-entered.

Timestamp. Every collapsed card shows when it was last touched. Not when the rep opened it. When something actually changed. The rep sees "Last touched: 12 days ago" on a card and knows it's going stale without anyone nagging them.

Days in stage. How long has this deal been sitting at this stage? Glanceable on the card. No chart, no dashboard. Just the number. The rep notices it themselves.

Honesty button. Self-reported risk, private to the rep. "I think this deal is in trouble." One click. Feeds scoring. Not visible to the manager unless the rep chooses. Solves the problem where reps sandpaper bad news because CRM data is performative.

Weak signal alerts. Rules that feed the alerts dropdown in the header: deal hasn't been touched in X days, close date pushed more than once, late-stage deal missing contacts, Signals boxes empty past a certain stage, meeting cadence dropped, email engagement declining. The alert doesn't pop up. It sits in the dropdown. The rep checks when they're ready.

Anti-Bloat Philosophy

No dashboards for reps. No pop-up notifications. No custom fields. No reporting at rep level. No gamification. No leaderboards. No "activity scores."

Let the rep notice it themselves. Respect their intelligence.

Every feature must pass one test: does this help the rep close a deal, or does this help the manager watch the rep? If it's the second one, it doesn't belong in the rep's view. Build it for the manager on their side of the API Bridge.

Email is Core

Deal-defining moments often happen in email threads, not recorded calls. The Ken's Roofing validation proved this: the pivotal confirmation that moved the deal forward came via email, not in any of the five recorded meetings. Email integration is core to the product, not an optional add-on.

Layer 1 AI reads email threads the same way it reads transcripts. It extracts contacts, next steps, objections, timeline clues, and competitive mentions. Email is a transcript that already exists in text form.

Implementation Requirements

CRM implementation should require closed-won and closed-lost deal exports (12-24 months) so AI calibrates to the customer's specific patterns, not generic best practices. "Companies like yours that lost deals had these patterns" is exponentially more powerful than "best practice says you should have executive access by stage 3."

Per-rep calibration develops over 6-12 months. The system learns each rep's individual bias pattern: who sandbags, who is overly optimistic, who forgets to update process information. This is the retention moat. After a year, the AI knows YOUR reps. Switching to a competitor means starting over.

Call Recording & Transcription Roadmap

Transcripts are the fuel. Without them, Layer 1 AI (the assistant) has nothing to read, Layer 2 AI (the analyst) has nothing to score, and the Signals framework runs on rep assertions instead of evidence. This section maps how every type of sales team gets call data into Deal Insight, from a solo rep with a cell phone to a 200-seat org running Gong.

Working prototype: DealInsight_CallRecorder_Prototype_v1.html — open in Chrome to test live recording
The product doesn't need to record the call. It needs the transcript. How it gets there is the customer's choice. Deal Insight meets them where they are.

The Maturity Ladder

Sales teams fall into five tiers of recording maturity. Deal Insight must serve all five on day one, not just the ones with enterprise budgets.

Tier 0: Nothing. No recording, no transcription, no call tools. The rep takes notes by hand or doesn't take notes at all. This is most small teams. Deal Insight serves them with manual input on the rep card (pain, contacts, next step, notes). The Signals framework still works. It just scores what the rep enters. This is the baseline, and it's already better than a spreadsheet.

Tier 1: Copy-paste. The rep uses any recording or transcription tool (Fathom, Otter, Zoom's built-in transcript, a phone recording app) and pastes the transcript into Deal Insight. This is what the Pipeline Tracker does today. Zero integration required. The AI reads the pasted transcript, populates card fields, and Signals scores the deal. Friction is real, but it works.

Tier 2: File upload. The rep drags an audio file or transcript file into the deal card. Deal Insight sends it to a transcription API (Deepgram, AssemblyAI, or Whisper), gets structured text back, and feeds it to Layer 1. Slightly less friction than copy-paste. Works for phone recordings, voice memos, and exported audio from any platform.

Tier 3: Browser recorder. A record button built directly into the deal card. The rep opens the card, hits Record, puts the phone on speaker (or shares screen audio), and Deal Insight captures audio from the browser microphone via the Web Audio API. When the call ends, the audio blob uploads to the transcription API automatically. Zero external tools. Zero dependencies. This is the "no excuses" tier. Build cost is minimal. The rep never leaves Deal Insight.

Tier 4: Platform integration. Deal Insight connects to the customer's existing call platform via API or webhook. Zoom, Microsoft Teams, Google Meet, Aircall, Dialpad, RingCentral, CloudTalk. When a call ends, the platform notifies Deal Insight, the recording downloads automatically, transcription runs, and the transcript lands on the deal card. The rep does nothing. This is the API Bridge (Section 4) in action.

Tier 5: Gong/Chorus. Enterprise teams already running conversation intelligence. Deal Insight pulls transcripts from Gong or Chorus via their APIs. The transcript feeds Layer 1 and Layer 2. Deal Insight doesn't compete with Gong on recording. It competes on what happens after the transcript exists: signal scoring, deal coaching, and rep-side intelligence that Gong doesn't do.

Tier 0 through Tier 5 all land in the same place: a transcript on the deal card, feeding the Signals framework. The input method changes. The intelligence doesn't.

What to Build and When

Phase 1 (Now, V1 Beta). Tier 0 and Tier 1 are already working. Manual input and copy-paste. No build required.

Phase 2 (Next, pre-launch). Tier 2 (file upload) and Tier 3 (browser recorder). The browser recorder is the priority. It gives Deal Insight a native recording story without depending on any third-party platform. Build estimate for John: 1-2 weeks for the browser recorder, 1 week for file upload and transcription API wiring. Total: 2-3 weeks.

Phase 3 (API Bridge, post-launch). Tier 4 (platform integrations). Zoom and Teams webhooks first, then cloud phone systems. This is the same API Bridge infrastructure that handles CRM sync, so the plumbing serves double duty. Build estimate: 2-4 weeks per platform, ongoing.

Phase 4 (Enterprise). Tier 5 (Gong/Chorus integration). Pull transcripts from their APIs. Lower priority because these customers already have transcription solved. The value prop for them is Signals scoring and coaching, not recording.

Legal Risks: Call Recording Consent

Recording laws vary by jurisdiction and this is the single biggest legal risk in the recording space. Deal Insight must handle this carefully.

One-party consent states (majority of US). Only one person on the call needs to know the call is being recorded. The rep counts. Most B2B sales calls fall here. This includes New York, Texas, Ohio, and most of the US.

Two-party (all-party) consent states. Everyone on the call must be informed. This includes California, Illinois, Pennsylvania, Florida, Washington, Maryland, and others. Reps calling into these states must disclose. "This call may be recorded for quality and training purposes" is standard.

International. GDPR (EU/UK) requires explicit consent and data processing agreements. Canada has similar rules. Australia requires one-party consent for private calls but stricter rules for business.

What Deal Insight should do:

  • Never auto-record without the rep explicitly starting it. The rep presses a button. That's consent on their end.
  • Display a clear disclaimer before recording starts: "Ensure all parties have been notified this call is being recorded."
  • Offer a "recording disclosure" audio snippet the rep can play at the start of the call, or a text template they can read.
  • Store recordings with encryption. Auto-delete after transcription completes (configurable retention). Don't hoard audio.
  • Include recording consent language in the Terms of Service. Make the customer responsible for local compliance, but make it easy for them to comply.
  • Flag two-party consent states in the UI if the prospect's area code or address is known.

Bot visibility. Tools like Fathom and Fireflies send a visible bot into video calls ("Fathom Notetaker has joined"). Some prospects find this off-putting, especially in healthcare, finance, legal, and senior executive conversations. Deal Insight's browser recorder avoids this entirely. No bot joins. The rep records from their own browser. This is a competitive advantage for privacy-conscious verticals.
